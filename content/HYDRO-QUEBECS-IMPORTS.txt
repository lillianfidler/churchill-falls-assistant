KEY FINDINGS SUMMARY:

1. DRAMATIC SHIFT IN IMPORT PATTERNS:
   - USA imports up 488.4% (July year-over-year)
   - USA imports up 339.5% (August year-over-year)
   - Provincial imports down 6.1% (July) and 9.4% (over 3 years)
   - HQ now importing from its traditional export market

2. PRICE ESCALATION FOR IMPORTS:
   - 2024 average import price from NY: 4.1¢/kWh
   - Jan-Aug 2025 average: 7.2¢/kWh
   - Increase of 75.6% year-over-year
   - Peak monthly prices reaching 22.5¢/kWh (Feb 2025)
   - Compare to CF contract price (indicated at bottom of chart as minimal)

3. HQ'S OWN REPORTING (Mid-year 2025):
   - Exports: ~6 TWh at 16¢/kWh (2025) vs 9¢/kWh (2024)
   - Imports: ~6 TWh at 5¢/kWh (2025) vs 3¢/kWh (2024)
   - HQ acknowledges "increase in electricity imports"
   - Strategic response to price fluctuations in New England market

4. EXPORT VS IMPORT BALANCE WITH NY:
   August 2025 comparison:
   - Exports to NY: up only 10.1% vs previous year
   - Imports from NY: up 339.5% vs previous year
   - Fundamental reversal of historical trading relationship

5. NEW ENGLAND MARKET VOLATILITY:
   - January-February 2025: Prices spiked to 18-19¢/kWh
   - Historical average (2020-2024): 7-8¢/kWh
   - March 2025: Sharp decline to 5-6¢/kWh
   - April-June 2025: Stabilized at 3-5¢/kWh
   - High winter volatility driving import costs

6. SEASONAL PATTERNS:
   - Strong winter peaks in provincial imports (likely CF)
   - Winter demand driving highest import prices from USA
   - Summer months showing lower but still elevated USA import volumes
   - Question raised: Do provincial import patterns match CF generation?

7. DATA TRANSPARENCY ISSUES:
   - Monthly CF import data not publicly available
   - ATIPPA request filed for monthly data back to January 2015
   - CER says all USA imports from NY; HQ says differently
   - HQ doesn't classify CF as "import" (due to ownership stake)

8. THE CF PRICE COMPARISON:
   - Chart notation shows CF price at bottom (minimal)
   - USA import prices 18-50+ times higher than CF price
   - CF price estimated at 0.2-0.4¢/kWh based on contract
   - USA prices ranging from 4¢/kWh to 22.5¢/kWh

================================================================================

STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS:

1. SUPPLY CONSTRAINT CRISIS:
   Quebec cannot meet its electricity needs from:
   - Its own HQ generation
   - Churchill Falls imports (at capacity)
   - Must turn to expensive USA imports

2. COST ESCALATION:
   - USA electricity costs 18-50+ times more than Churchill Falls
   - Average import cost from NY rose 75.6% in one year
   - Winter price spikes reaching 22.5¢/kWh
   - Threatening Quebec's low-cost advantage

3. MARKET POSITION REVERSAL:
   - Quebec traditionally a net exporter to USA
   - Now increasingly dependent on USA imports
   - Imports from primary export market up 488%
   - Fundamental restructuring of electricity trade

4. CF DEPENDENCY HIGHLIGHTED:
   - Provincial imports declining while USA imports surge
   - CF at maximum capacity - cannot provide more
   - Expensive USA power filling the gap
   - Demonstrates CF's irreplaceable value

5. PROFITABILITY STRATEGY:
   HQ's approach:
   - Buy low (5¢/kWh imports in Q2)
   - Sell high (16¢/kWh exports in Q1)
   - Use USA market opportunistically
   - Enabled by CF's cheap baseload

6. 2025 BUYING AND SELLING PATTERN:
   - Q1 (Cold): Sell 6 TWh at 16¢/kWh (exports)
   - Q2 (Mild): Buy 6 TWh at 5¢/kWh (imports)
   - Both volumes approximately equal
   - Net profit from arbitrage
   - But increasing import dependency

1. Why are provincial imports declining?
   - Is CF reducing output?

   - Data transparency needed

   - Does it match the seasonal peaks shown?
   - Why won't NHL and HQ release this data?
   - ATIPPA request pending

3. Is the USA import surge temporary or structural?
   - Climate change increasing Quebec demand?
   - Quebec generation insufficient?
   - Long-term supply gap emerging?

4. What happens when CF contract expires (2041)?
   - Current imports from CF: ~31.5 TWh annually
   - Replacement cost at USA rates: Billions more annually
   - Is Quebec preparing for this?

5. Can Quebec maintain its competitive advantage?
   - Cheapest rates in G7 depend on cheap CF power
   - USA imports undermining cost advantage
   - How sustainable is current model?

THE VALUE OF CHURCHILL FALLS - QUANTIFIED:

Based on this evidence:

VOLUME:
- CF provides ~31.5 TWh annually to Quebec
- Represents 80.6% of Quebec's provincial imports
- At maximum capacity - no additional supply available

PRICE COMPARISON:
- CF contract price: ~0.2-0.4¢/kWh (estimated)
- USA import price 2024: 4.1¢/kWh (10-20x more)
- USA import price Jan-Aug 2025: 7.2¢/kWh (18-36x more)
- USA peak prices Feb 2025: 22.5¢/kWh (56-112x more)

ANNUAL VALUE DIFFERENTIAL:
If Quebec had to replace CF's 31.5 TWh with USA imports:

At 2024 average (4.1¢/kWh):
- Cost with CF: $63-126 million
- Cost with USA: $1.29 billion
- Annual difference: $1.16-1.23 billion

At Jan-Aug 2025 average (7.2¢/kWh):
- Cost with CF: $63-126 million
- Cost with USA: $2.27 billion
- Annual difference: $2.14-2.20 billion

At winter peak (22.5¢/kWh):
- Cost with CF: $63-126 million
- Cost with USA: $7.09 billion
- Annual difference: $6.96-7.02 billion

This quantifies why Newfoundland and Labrador views the current CF contract as 
dramatically undervaluing the electricity.

================================================================================

TRADING PATTERN TRANSFORMATION:

HISTORICAL PATTERN (Pre-2024):
- Quebec: Net exporter to USA
- USA imports: Essentially zero
- CF power enabled profitable exports
- Quebec maintained lowest rates in G7

NEW PATTERN (2024-2025):
- Quebec: Increasingly importing from USA
- USA imports up 339-488%
- CF at capacity - cannot meet Quebec's growing needs
- Quebec paying 18-50x more for USA power than CF power
- Competitive advantage at risk

This represents one of the most dramatic shifts in North American electricity 
trade patterns in recent decades.

CONCLUSION:

The evidence overwhelmingly demonstrates:

1. Quebec faces a growing electricity supply gap
2. Churchill Falls is at maximum capacity and cannot provide more
3. Quebec is filling the gap with USA imports costing 18-50+ times more
4. This represents a fundamental reversal of historical trading patterns
5. The true value of Churchill Falls electricity is revealed by market 
   alternatives
6. Quebec's competitive advantage depends on maintaining access to CF's 
   ultra-cheap power
7. The current CF contract represents a massive subsidy from NL to Quebec
8. When the contract expires in 2041, replacement costs will be billions more 
   annually

The data provides strong empirical support for Newfoundland and Labrador's 
position that Churchill Falls electricity is dramatically undervalued in the 
current contractual arrangement.
